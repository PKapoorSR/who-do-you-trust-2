04 — Course Concepts

Five Frameworks, One Argument

Five tools for analyzing how fitness influencers build trust. Each one reveals something different about why we believe who we believe.

Framework 01

Source Credibility Theory

Hovland, C.I., Janis, I.L., & Kelley, H.H. (1953). Communication and Persuasion. Yale University Press.

When we decide whether to trust someone, we judge three things at once: how much they know, how honest they seem, and how much we like them. The key point is that none of these require the person to actually be right. Looking credible and being credible are not the same thing.

Framework 03

Healthism

Crawford, R. (1980). Healthism and the medicalization of everyday life. International Journal of Health Services, 10(3), 365–388.

Healthism is the idea that being healthy is a personal moral duty. Robert Crawford coined the term in 1980, arguing this thinking ignores the social and economic reasons people struggle. All four creators push this idea in different ways. Jeff optimizes with science. Togi makes the body extreme. Bro Science mocks gym culture while being completely inside it. JPG frames health as cosmic alignment.

Jeff — Rational Healthism

Right information plus right habits equals results. Useful and research-grounded, but it ignores that not everyone has equal access to time, food, or money.

Togi — Transgressive Healthism

Togi breaks every conventional health rule but still treats his body as his main project. He swapped wellness for authenticity. The body is still the point.

Bro Science — Satirical Healthism

The joke only works if you're already that person. His audience laughs at themselves. The parody is still part of the culture it mocks.

JPG — Spiritual Healthism

Health as cosmic alignment. If you're not getting results, your frequency is off. Failure is not just physical. It is a spiritual one.

Framework 04

Cultural Production & Reproduction

Bourdieu, P. (1993). The Field of Cultural Production. Polity. / Hall, S. (1980). Encoding/Decoding. In Culture, Media, Language. Hutchinson.

Stuart Hall's encoding/decoding model: creators put meaning into content, and audiences read it back out but not always the same way. Bourdieu looked at culture as a competition. On YouTube and TikTok, the algorithm runs that competition and rewards shocking, emotional content over careful, accurate content. Even creators trying to be scientific get pushed toward drama. Jeff Nippard packages research as a YouTube show because he has to.

The Algorithm as Field

YouTube rewards watch time and emotional reactions. Shocking content spreads further than accurate content. Togi's videos are built for this. Jeff's succeed in spite of it.

Reproduction of Norms

Every creator reinforces the same norm: the body is a project to manage. Bro Science jokes about gym obsession but the joke only works on people who are already obsessed. Subverting an idea from inside it often ends up reinforcing it.

Framework 05

Visual Rhetoric

Foss, S. (2004). Framing the study of visual rhetoric. Defining Visual Rhetorics. / Barthes, R. (1977). Rhetoric of the Image. In Image-Music-Text. Fontana.

Visuals make arguments before any words do. Each creator's visual style is a trust argument that lands before a single fact is stated. Jeff's lab-style graphics say this is rigorous. Togi's handheld footage says this is raw and real. Dom's comedy staging says don't take this literally. JPG's glowing aesthetic says this is a safe, open space. The visuals shape what the audience is prepared to believe before evaluation begins.

Jeff — Scientific Visual Register

White backgrounds, on-screen citations, split-screen comparisons borrow the visual language of academic presentations. This is not science. It is the look of science.

Togi — Authenticity Visual Register

Handheld footage, raw lighting, direct address to camera signal nothing is hidden. But "authentic" is also a style. It takes deliberate choices to look like nothing is staged.

Bro Science — Comedy Visual Register

Exaggerated performance, comedy-sketch cuts, bright color grading signal this is a joke. When Dom says something true, the format gives it built-in cover.

JPG — Spiritual Visual Register

Soft focus, pastel colors, slow panning shots from wellness and yoga culture signal enter with openness, not skepticism. A visual invitation to stop asking questions.

Project Findings

Six Takeaways

What comparing four creators through five frameworks actually showed.

01
Credibility Is Performed Before It Is Earned

Jeff's citation graphics, Togi's raw footage, Dom's comedy setup, JPG's soft glow all make a trust argument before anything is said. The packaging shapes what you're ready to believe.

02
Expertise and Appeal Are Inversely Distributed

Jeff scores 90 on expertise and 65 on appeal. Togi scores 88 on appeal and 22 on expertise. Being the most knowledgeable and being the most popular are not the same thing. They may work against each other.

03
Authenticity Is a Credibility Strategy, Not Its Opposite

Togi's openness is as calculated as Jeff's citations. Both are trust strategies. The difference is that Togi's approach can't be fact-checked. "This happened to me" doesn't have a source.

04
Satire Cannot Escape What It Critiques

The joke only works if the audience is already inside gym culture. When you parody something for people who love it, you reinforce it at the same time.

05
The Algorithm Rewards the Least Verifiable Content

Engagement metrics favor shocking, emotional content. Togi's videos are built for that. Jeff succeeds in spite of it. His audience is actively looking for him, not stumbling on him through recommendations.

06
Healthism Adapts to Every Credibility Register

Four completely different styles, same core message: health is your responsibility and your body is the main project. The idea shows up in forms that look like opposites, which is why it's hard to push back on.
